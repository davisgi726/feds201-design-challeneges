Designing Parts That Actually Print

FDM 3D printing builds parts one layer at a time from the bottom up. This sounds simple, but it means every decision you make in CAD has a consequence on the printer. This page teaches you the rules — so your design works the first time.

⭐

Print Orientation — The Most Important Decision You Make

FDM printers deposit plastic in horizontal layers. Each layer bonds to the one below it — but that bond is weaker than the plastic itself. This means a printed part is anisotropic: stronger in the XY plane (across layers) than in Z (between layers). How you orient a part determines where it is strong, where it is weak, and whether it needs supports.

✅ Good Orientation

Large flat face on the bed. Layers stack in the direction of least stress. No overhangs. Maximum contact with bed = better adhesion. No supports needed.

❌ Bad Orientation

Part balanced on a small face or edge. Overhangs in critical areas. Layers stack across a stress direction. Needs supports that leave marks and take time to remove.

💡 The Chicken Zombie rule: Every part was designed so its largest flat face sits on the bed. The body prints flat. The head prints flat. The beak prints flat. The legs print vertically because they are tall and thin — that IS the flat face for that geometry.
📐
The 45° Overhang Rule

Plastic extruded into open air with nothing below it will sag, curl, or fail. Most FDM printers can handle overhangs up to 45° from vertical without supports because each new layer still has enough of the layer below it to bond to. Beyond 45°, you need supports — or you redesign the geometry.

⚠️ Design rule: Keep all overhangs at 45° or less from vertical. For Minecraft-style box geometry, this is easy — all faces are 0° or 90°. The challenge comes when you add angled features like chamfers, dovetail slots, or diagonal cuts.
🌉
Bridging — Spanning a Gap Without Supports

A bridge is a horizontal span between two supported walls. Unlike an overhang (which hangs off one edge), a bridge has support on both sides. FDM printers can bridge short gaps — typically up to 50mm (2 inches) cleanly — because the extruder pulls the plastic taut like a cable between two anchor points. Beyond that, the middle sags.

💡 The eye sockets on the Chicken Zombie head are bridged — two walls with a short horizontal span across the top of the eye recess. At Minecraft scale this is well under 50mm, so it prints cleanly without supports.
🔩
Three Joint Types — Designing Without Fasteners

The Chicken Zombie uses three distinct joint types, each designed directly in CAD. No screws, no bolts. The geometry itself creates the connection. You'll use at least two of these in your design.

1 — Straight Slide Fit

A pin or tab slides into a matching slot. Used for the legs into the body. Add clearance on all mating faces so the part doesn't bind.

2 — Dovetail Slide

An angled "wedge" profile that locks the part against pulling straight out. Used for the beak and wattle. The angled faces provide mechanical retention.

3 — Pin & Socket (Rotating)

A cylindrical pin sits in a matching hole. If clearance is correct, the part rotates freely. Used for the spinning zombie head.

FDM Clearance Specifications — Use These Numbers Straight slide fit: 0.012" gap per face (0.006" per side radial)
Dovetail slide: 0.012" gap on angled faces (45° standard angle)
Pin diameter: 0.369" → Socket diameter: 0.375" = 0.006" radial clearance

These are tuned for FDM PLA on a well-calibrated printer. Tighter = binding. Looser = sloppy fit.

Scaling from a Pixel Grid

Minecraft characters are defined on a pixel grid published on the Minecraft Wiki. Each pixel maps to a real dimension. The Chicken Zombie used 1 pixel = 1mm as the base scale, then the whole model was scaled up to get the desired print size while staying within filament budget.

Scaling Workflow 1. Count pixels for each dimension on the Minecraft Wiki texture
2. Set base scale: 1 pixel = 1 mm in Onshape
3. Model at base scale
4. Scale entire model: desired_size ÷ base_size = scale_factor

Example: Chicken body is 9px wide at base scale = 9mm
Desired width = 45mm → scale factor = 45 ÷ 9 = 5×
Apply 5× scale to entire model in Onshape

All joints scale with the model — recalculate clearances after scaling
⚠️ After scaling, check your clearances. If you modeled a 0.012" gap at base scale and then scale the model 5×, your gap becomes 0.060" — far too loose. Either model clearances at the final scale, or add clearances AFTER scaling.
💰
Estimating Filament Cost

Filament is sold by weight (typically 1kg spools). Your slicer software will tell you the estimated filament volume or weight for each part. Use this to estimate cost — a real engineering constraint on any fabrication project.

Cost Calculation Volume (cm³) = from Onshape mass properties or slicer estimate
Mass (g) = Volume × density ← PLA density ≈ 1.24 g/cm³
Cost = Mass (g) × price per gram

Price per gram = spool cost ÷ spool weight
Example: $20 spool ÷ 1000g = $0.020 per gram

Look up your specific filament brand and color — prices vary $15–$35 per kg
🔑 Design constraint: Your total model must cost less than $2.00 in filament across all parts. This forces you to think about wall thickness, infill, and part size — exactly like a real product engineer.
🧩
How to Decompose a Character Into Parts

The key question is: where do the color boundaries fall? Each region that needs a different filament color becomes a separate part. Each part must be independently printable without supports. The joints between parts are designed at the color boundaries.

Full exploded view — each color region is a separate printable part

Transparent front view — shows how parts nest and stack in assembly

Step 1 — Find Color Boundaries

Look at your character reference. Draw lines where colors change. Each region between lines is a candidate for a separate part.

Step 2 — Check Printability

For each candidate part: can it sit flat? Does it have overhangs beyond 45°? If yes — redesign the boundary or split it differently.

Step 3 — Design the Joint

At each boundary, choose your joint type. Sliding parts → straight slot. Parts that need retention → dovetail. Rotating parts → pin and socket.

Step 4 — Add Clearance Last

Model parts to the exact nominal dimension first. Add clearance (0.012") as the final step on each mating face. This keeps your model clean and easy to edit.

Model It in Onshape

Follow the steps below. Model each part in its own Part Studio tab so you can control appearance separately. Assemble in a final Assembly tab to verify fit.

1
Step 1 — Document Setup
  1. Create a new Onshape document. Name it: FEDS201_[LastName]_[CharacterName]
  2. Set units to Inches (your clearance specs are in inches). You can mix mm dimensions for the body with inch clearances — Onshape handles both.
  3. Create one Part Studio tab per part. Name each tab by part: "Body", "Head", "Beak", etc. This lets you control appearance color separately for each part. Part Studios tutorial →
2
Step 2 — Model the Base Part (Body)
  1. In the Body Part Studio, click Sketch → Top plane. Draw your body footprint using rectangles. Each pixel = 1mm at base scale. Sketching tutorial →
  2. Extrude the profile to the body height (in mm at 1px = 1mm scale). This is your base block.
  3. Add any recesses or features using Extrude → Remove. For slide slots, sketch the slot profile on the appropriate face and remove through the wall.
  4. Set the part appearance: right-click the part in the Parts list → Appearance → choose the correct filament color.
3
Step 3 — Model the Pin & Socket (Rotating Joint)
  1. On the part that gets the pin stub (e.g. the head underside): sketch a circle, diameter = 0.369", centered on the face. Extrude the circle outward (away from the part) to your desired peg height — typically 0.25"–0.375". This is your pin.
  2. On the part that gets the socket (e.g. the body top): sketch a circle, diameter = 0.375", at the matching position. Extrude → Remove to the socket depth (slightly deeper than the pin height so the pin doesn't bottom out). This is your socket.
  3. Add a small fillet (0.02") to the base of the pin to reduce stress concentration and help it slide into the socket smoothly.
💡 Test the fit before printing everything. Export just the head and the top of the body as a small test print. If the pin binds, increase socket diameter by 0.005" and reprint. If it's too loose, decrease socket by 0.005". Iterate before committing to the full print.
4
Step 4 — Model the Dovetail Joint
  1. On the face that receives the dovetail (e.g. chicken head front face): sketch the dovetail pocket profile. Draw a trapezoid — wider at the back, narrower at the opening — with 45° angled sides. The opening width should be the beak width minus 0.012" per side. Sketch tools →
  2. Extrude the dovetail pocket into the head face to the pocket depth (typically the beak depth minus 0.050" so the beak bottoms out with a small gap).
  3. On the beak part: model the beak block at nominal size first. Then sketch the dovetail tail profile on the back face — matching the pocket shape but 0.012" smaller on all angled faces. Extrude to match pocket depth.
  4. Key check: The dovetail tail must be able to slide into the pocket from the side — it cannot be inserted straight in. Make sure you have access to slide it in during assembly.
⚠️ Dovetails are the hardest joint to get right on first try. Model the pocket first, then derive the tail from the pocket dimensions. Always add 0.012" clearance on the angled faces — not the flat back face.
5
Step 5 — Scale the Model
  1. Once all parts are modeled at 1px = 1mm base scale, check your total height. Calculate your scale factor: desired height ÷ current height.
  2. In each Part Studio, use Part Studio → Scale (or create a configuration variable). Apply your scale factor uniformly to X, Y, and Z.
  3. After scaling, fix your clearances. The pin and socket dimensions will have scaled with the model — replace them with the correct values: pin = 0.369", socket = 0.375". Edit the sketch dimensions directly after scaling.
  4. Verify the largest part is still within 150 × 150 × 150 mm. If not, reduce your scale factor.
6
Step 6 — Assembly & Verification
  1. Create an Assembly tab. Insert all parts. Fix the body part. Assemblies tutorial →
  2. Apply Fastened mates for static joints (legs, beak, wattle). Apply a Revolute mate for the rotating head (select the pin axis).
  3. Test the Revolute mate: right-click the head → Animate. The head should rotate freely around the pin axis. If it collides with the body in any rotation, you need clearance between the head bottom and the body top.
  4. Use Onshape Mass Properties (right-click part → Mass Properties) to get the volume of each part. Calculate your actual filament cost and compare to your $2.00 budget.
7
Step 7 — Export STL for Each Part
  1. In each Part Studio, right-click the part → Export → Format: STL → Units: Inch → Resolution: Fine.
  2. Name each file: FEDS201_[LastName]_[CharacterName]_[PartName].stl
  3. Open each STL in your slicer (PrusaSlicer, Bambu Studio, etc.). Orient the part correctly — flat face down. Confirm zero supports are required. If supports appear, fix the orientation.
  4. Record the slicer's estimated filament weight for each part. Sum them up and recalculate your actual cost.
