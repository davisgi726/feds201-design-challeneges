The Engineering Design Process

Before you design anything, you have to understand the problem — and the problem is almost never exactly what someone first tells you. This challenge teaches you the process engineers use to go from a fuzzy complaint to a tested solution.

Stage 1 — The Client Interview

A client tells you their problem in the language of their experience, not in engineering terms. Your job is to listen carefully, ask clarifying questions, and translate what they say into something you can actually design to.

What the client says

"My cat keeps losing her toys under the oven and fridge. It's really annoying and she cries at me to get them out."

What an engineer hears

There is a gap between an appliance and the floor (or adjacent surface). Small objects pass through this gap and become inaccessible. The client wants this to stop.

⚠️ Stated problem vs. root cause. The client said "cat keeps losing toys." That's a symptom. The root cause is a physical gap that allows objects below a certain size to pass through. You could solve the stated problem by removing all toys — but that doesn't address the root cause. Always design to the root cause.

Clarifying Questions — What To Ask

After the first statement, a good engineer asks questions that reveal dimensions, constraints, and priorities. For this problem:

Good Clarifying Questions How wide is the gap between the oven/fridge and the floor?
How wide is the gap between the oven/fridge and the adjacent cabinet?
What size are the toys that fall through? (measure the smallest one)
Does the solution need to be removable? (e.g. for cleaning)
Is the oven freestanding or built-in? Does it need to slide out?
What materials are acceptable? (adhesive OK? drilling OK?)
Is aesthetics a concern — does it need to match the kitchen?
What is the budget?
📌 Notice that none of these questions mention a solution. You are gathering information about the problem space, not proposing answers. The moment you mention a solution in the client interview, you've anchored the conversation and you'll miss requirements.
📋
Stage 2 — SMART Requirements

A requirement is a statement of what the design must do — not how it does it. "The design must block toys" is not a requirement. It's a wish. A SMART requirement is testable: you can look at the final design and definitively say pass or fail.

LetterMeaningBad ExampleGood Example
SSpecific"Should stop toys going under""Shall prevent objects ≥ 38mm diameter from passing through the gap"
MMeasurable"Should be cheap""Total material cost shall not exceed $5.00"
AAchievable"Shall be invisible""Shall be less than 10mm tall above the floor surface"
RRelevant"Shall be waterproof to 30m""Shall be cleanable with a damp cloth"
TTime-bound"Should be done eventually""Shall be installable in under 10 minutes without tools"
🔑 The test: Can you look at your finished design and say definitively "pass" or "fail" for this requirement? If not, rewrite it until you can.
⚖️
Stage 3 — The Weighted Decision Matrix

A decision matrix is a structured way to compare multiple concepts against the same criteria. It prevents you from picking your favorite idea and then reverse-engineering reasons why it's best.

Criteria come from requirements

Each evaluation criterion maps directly to a requirement. If it's not in your requirements, it shouldn't be in your matrix — and if it's in your matrix, it should be in your requirements.

Weights reflect priority

Not all requirements are equal. A safety requirement might be weight 5. An aesthetic preference might be weight 1. Weights force you to decide what matters most before you score anything.

How the Matrix Works Weighted Score = Score × Weight (for each criterion)
Total Score = Sum of all Weighted Scores (for each concept)

Score range: 1 (worst) to 5 (best)
Weight range: 1 (nice to have) to 5 (critical / must-have)

Highest total score = recommended concept
BUT: if the winner scores 1 on a weight-5 criterion, reconsider.

Defining Your Scoring Rubric — Before You Score Anything

The most important step in a decision matrix is defining what each score means for each criterion before you look at any concept. If cost is a criterion, what does a 1 mean? What does a 5 mean? Write this down first. Otherwise you'll unconsciously score your preferred concept higher.

Example Scoring Rubric — Cost Criterion Score 1: Total cost > $15
Score 2: Total cost $10–$15
Score 3: Total cost $5–$10
Score 4: Total cost $2–$5
Score 5: Total cost < $2

Now when you score a concept, you look up what it costs, find that row, and that's the score. No judgment. No bias.
⚠️ The matrix doesn't make the decision for you. It informs the decision. If the matrix picks Concept B but you have a strong engineering reason to prefer Concept A, document that reason. But you have to be honest about whether it's an engineering reason or just preference.
🖨️
Stage 4 — Prototyping & What You Learn

A prototype is not a finished product. It is a physical question: "Does this concept work the way I think it does?" The goal is to answer that question as cheaply and quickly as possible. FDM 3D printing is ideal for this — fast, cheap, iteratable.

What a prototype tests

Fit: does it fit the gap? Function: does it actually stop toys? Assembly: can you install it without tools? These are things your matrix estimated — the prototype gives you real data.

What you learn from failure

A prototype that fails is not a waste. It tells you which requirement your estimate was wrong about. That goes back into your matrix rubric and potentially changes the winning concept.

🔑 The loop: Prototype → Test against requirements → Update rubric scores with real data → Does the matrix winner change? → If yes, iterate. This is why requirements and the matrix exist: they give you a framework to learn from prototyping, not just a one-time decision tool.

Page 5 of 6 · Prototype CAD

Model Your Selected Concept

Build a prototype model of your winning concept in Onshape. The goal is not a finished product — it's a physical test of your key assumptions. What does the prototype need to prove?

🎯
What Your Prototype Must Test
1
Step 1 — Set Up Your Document
  1. Create a new Onshape document. Name it: FEDS201_[LastName]_CatToyPrototype
  2. Set units to match your measurements — Inches if you measured in inches, mm if you measured in mm.
  3. Write your key measurements as a comment at the top of your first sketch so they're always visible: gap width, appliance height, toy minimum diameter. Part Studios reference →
2
Step 2 — Model the Gap (Reference Geometry)

Just like the tennis ball challenge — model the thing you're designing FOR before you design the solution. This ensures your part is sized correctly.

  1. Sketch a rectangle representing the appliance footprint on the Top plane. Extrude it to the appliance height. Set appearance to gray, transparency to 70%. This is your reference appliance.
  2. Add a second extrusion representing the floor section adjacent to the appliance. The gap between them should match your measured gap width.
  3. Rename parts: "Appliance (Reference)" and "Floor (Reference)". These are visual guides only — you'll suppress them for export.
3
Step 3 — Model Your Solution

The geometry depends on your chosen concept. Use the guidance below for the most common solution types.

  1. Gap cover / barrier strip: Sketch a profile on the Front plane. The width should equal your gap width plus overlap onto each surface (typically 10–15mm each side). Extrude to a depth that provides stability. Add a slight taper or chamfer on the leading edge so it guides rather than catches. Extrude tutorial →
  2. Brush seal / flexible barrier: Model the rigid mounting channel. The brush element itself can be represented as a solid fin for the prototype — you'll specify the material in your report. Focus on modeling the channel geometry accurately so you can test the fit.
  3. Retrieval tool / wand: Model the handle and the retrieval tip (hook, magnet housing, or scoop). The tip geometry is what you're testing — make it long enough to reach under the appliance at your measured clearance height.
  4. Raised leg / foot pad: If raising the appliance to make the gap accessible, model one foot pad. It must support the appliance weight — add thickness accordingly. Check that your raised height doesn't cause instability.
  5. Any concept: Once modeled, visually verify it fits against your reference geometry. Does it span the gap? Does it clear the appliance? Is it small enough to print on your bed?
⚠️ Size check: Keep your prototype under 150mm in any dimension so it fits on any school printer. If your full solution needs to be longer, print a representative 100mm section — enough to test fit and function.
4
Step 4 — Export & Test Plan
  1. Right-click your solution part → Export → STL → Fine resolution → Inch or mm. Suppress reference geometry first.
  2. Open in your slicer. Orient for best print results — no supports if possible.
  3. Before printing, write your test plan below. What exactly will you do with the prototype once it's printed?
