The Physics of Keeping Things Still

Before you design anything, you need to understand the forces at work. This page covers the concepts you'll use to design a holder that actually keeps a tennis ball from rolling off your desk. Read through all sections before moving on.

⚽
Why Does a Ball Roll?

A sphere resting on a flat surface has only one point of contact. Any horizontal force — a vibration, a bump, even air movement — creates a net torque around that single contact point. With no restoring force to oppose it, the ball rolls freely. Your job is to create a geometry that provides multiple contact points, distributing the ball's weight and creating a stable equilibrium.

Unstable Equilibrium

Ball on flat surface. One contact point. Any small disturbance causes the ball to roll away. The system does not return to rest on its own.

Stable Equilibrium

Ball in a holder. Multiple contact points or a shaped cradle. A small disturbance is countered by restoring forces — the ball returns to its resting position.

⬇
Center of Gravity & Stability

Every object's weight acts through its Center of Gravity (CoG) — the single point where gravity appears to act on the entire mass. For a uniform sphere like a tennis ball, the CoG is exactly at the geometric center.

A system is stable when the CoG is low and directly above the base of support. A holder works by dropping the ball's CoG below the rim of the cradle — the ball would have to rise to escape, which requires energy it doesn't have.

Key Rule: Stability Triangle / Base of Support Stability increases when:
→ CoG height is LOW relative to the base
→ Contact points are SPREAD APART
→ The CoG projects INSIDE the base of support polygon
📐
Contact Geometry — How Your Holder Touches the Ball

How your holder meets the sphere determines how stable it is. Three common approaches:

Conical Cradle (V-Groove)

Two angled surfaces meet to form a V. The ball rests in a ring of contact. Very stable. Easy to manufacture. Classic design for shaft holders and ball rests.

Concave Spherical Cup

A matching radius carved out to hold the sphere. Maximum contact area. Requires the internal radius ≥ ball radius. Slightly harder to model in CAD.

3-Point Contact (Pegs/Posts)

Three or more posts arranged in a circle. Ball rests on the tops. Easy to 3D print. Stability depends on post spacing — too close and the ball can escape.

Ring Support

A flat ring with an inner diameter smaller than the ball diameter. Ball rests on the ring edge. Simple, printable, but can tip sideways if the ring is thin.

Geometry Check — Ring / Cradle Opening Diameter For the ball to sit IN the holder (not on top):

D_opening < D_ball

For a 3-point peg holder, the ball rests at height h above the peg base:
h = R_ball − √(R_ball² − R_peg_circle²)

where R_peg_circle = radius of the circle the pegs sit on

🔩
Forces Your Holder Must Handle

Normal Force (N)

The holder pushes back on the ball perpendicular to the contact surface. At each contact point, N is directed toward the ball's center.

Friction Force (f)

Opposes motion along the contact surface. Higher friction coefficient (rubber, felt) = better grip. f ≤ μN where μ is the friction coefficient.

Gravity (mg)

Acts downward through the ball's CoG. Your holder must provide equal and opposite vertical reaction forces summing to mg.

Moment Arms

If contact forces don't pass through the CoG, they create moments. Good holder design ensures contact forces sum to zero net moment in all directions.

Step 1 — Set Up Your Document
  1. Go to onshape.com and log in to your account. Create a new document. Name it: FEDS201_[YourLastName]_TennisBallHolder
  2. In the Part Studio, set units to Inches: click the unit display at the bottom-right of the screen → select Inch.
  3. Rename your Part Studio tab (at the bottom) to "Holder".
2
Step 2 — Model the Tennis Ball (Reference Body)

Model the ball first as a reference so your holder can be designed around it. You'll suppress this body for the final export.

  1. Click Sketch → select the Front plane.
  2. Draw a circle centered on the origin. Set the diameter to your measured average value (from your Requirements page). Add a diameter dimension.
  3. Exit sketch. Click Revolve → select the circle profile → set the revolve axis to the vertical centerline → revolve 360°. This creates your reference sphere.
  4. Right-click the sphere in the Parts list → Rename it "Tennis Ball (Reference)". Right-click again → Appearance → set it to yellow or bright green so it's easy to identify.
💡 Using the actual ball geometry means you can visually verify that your holder makes contact at the right points. You can suppress/hide the ball body when exporting your holder STL.
3
Step 3 — Model Your Holder

Use your ideation dimensions from Page 3. Choose the path that matches your contact geometry approach.

  1. V-Groove / Ring approach: Sketch a profile on the Right or Front plane that defines the outer shape and the cradle groove. Use Revolve to create the body, then use a Boolean Subtract if needed for the cradle cut. Revolve tutorial →
  2. 3-Post approach: Extrude a flat base first. Then sketch a circle pattern at your desired peg circle radius. Extrude the peg circles to the correct height. Use the Circular Pattern feature to replicate posts. Circular pattern →
  3. Any approach: Add a fillet (minimum R = 0.03 in) to any sharp external edges to reduce print failure risk and improve safety.
  4. Optional — add your team number or your name using the Text tool in a sketch, then Extrude it as a 0.04 in raised or debossed feature.

3b
Step 3b — Verify Fit in an Assembly

Don't just eyeball it in the Part Studio — assemble the parts and use mates to confirm the ball actually seats correctly. This is exactly the workflow you'll use for every subsystem on the robot.

💡 In Onshape, an Assembly is a separate tab where you bring parts together and constrain their positions using mates. A mate defines a physical relationship between two surfaces — the same way a real bolt constrains a real bracket.
  1. At the bottom of your document, click the ＋ tab button → select Assembly. Rename the tab "Verification Assembly". Onshape Assemblies intro →
  2. Click Insert (top toolbar) → select your Holder part from the Parts list → click to place it. Then insert the Tennis Ball (Reference) part the same way. Click the green checkmark to close the Insert panel.
  3. Right-click the Holder in the assembly tree → select Fix. This locks the holder to the origin so it doesn't move when you mate the ball to it.
  4. Click Mate in the toolbar → select Tangent mate. Click the outer spherical surface of the ball as the first selection, then click one of the contact surfaces of your holder (the angled face, post top, or ring edge) as the second. Click the green checkmark to apply.
  5. Interpret the result:
    ✅ Ball seats inside the holder with the equator above the rim — geometry is correct.
    ⚠️ Mate solves but ball floats above the holder — opening is too large; the ball is resting on the outer rim edge rather than dropping in. Reduce your opening diameter.
    ❌ Mate fails or ball intersects (clips through) the holder — opening is too small or geometry conflict. Review your cradle dimensions.
  6. If you needed to change any dimensions, go back to the Part Studio, edit the sketch or feature dimension, and the Assembly will update automatically. Re-check the mate result.
  7. When the assembly looks correct, set a good view angle (3/4 isometric works well) — this is the screenshot you'll upload below.
⚙️ Tangent mate tip: For a V-groove or concave cup, select the angled face or curved interior surface — not the flat top of the holder. For a 3-post design, apply a Tangent mate to each post top individually (three mates total). The ball will fully constrain after enough mates are applied.
4
Step 4 — Export Your STL
📋 Export only the holder part — not the reference ball.
  1. In the Part Studio, right-click the holder part in the Parts panel (left sidebar) → Select "Export…"
  2. In the export dialog: Format → STL. Units → Inch. Resolution → Fine. Click Export. The file downloads to your computer.
  3. Name the file: FEDS201_[LastName]_TennisBallHolder.stl
  4. You can also share a link to your Onshape document: click Share (top right) → set access to "Anyone with the link can view" → copy the link and paste it in the field below.
